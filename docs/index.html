The $1.1 Trillion Question: Why Capital Gains Tax Reform Can't Wait
Expand

Every year, the wealthiest Americans legally eliminate hundreds of billions of dollars in capital gains from their tax obligations. Not through exotic offshore schemes or illegal tax evasion—but through a structural feature of the tax code that rewards sophisticated portfolio management with a tax rate roughly half what ordinary workers pay. This analysis quantifies that gap and shows what closing it would look like.

Section 1The Scale of the Problem

In tax year 2021, American taxpayers reported $1.92 trillion in net long-term capital gains on 20.5 million Schedule D filings. That figure alone exceeds the GDP of all but a handful of nations. But the number you don't see on the IRS Statistics of Income tables—the gross gains hidden by tax-loss harvesting—tells the real story.

Our model indicates that the net long-term capital gains reported in IRS tables substantially understate the total volume of realized gains. Before tax-loss harvesting and offsetting, gross gains are materially larger. Across all income levels, investors deliberately realize losses to offset realized gains, reducing the amount of capital income subject to tax. This behavior is entirely lawful under current statute. However, by narrowing the taxable base relative to economic gains realized, it materially reduces federal revenue.

$1.1 trillion Estimated additional federal revenue in TY2021 from eliminating preferential treatment for gains held under 5 years

That figure—over one trillion dollars in TY2021 alone—is not a theoretical maximum. It is the result of a five-step microsimulation applied to actual IRS data at all 19 income levels, using real tax bracket schedules and calibrated loss ratios. The model accounts for the $3,000 annual cap on capital loss deductions against ordinary income (IRC §1211(b)), the composition of gains by holding period, and the return-count-weighted marginal rates across all filing statuses. Even in the 2022 market correction, when gains fell to $1.35 trillion, the reform still generated $718 billion—equivalent to 28% of that year's $2.6 trillion in individual income tax collections.

Section 2Where the Money Is

The concentration of capital gains income in America is extreme, and growing. In 2021, filers with AGI above $2 million held 61.2% of all long-term gains, despite representing a tiny fraction of total filers. The $10 million-and-above cohort alone held 41.9% of all long-term gains—$805.6 billion in net reported gains from just 40,444 returns. See: Capital Gains Concentration

40,444 filers reported $805.6 billion in long-term gains. That is $19.9 million per return— more than most Americans will earn in a lifetime.

This concentration is not merely an artifact of income inequality. It reflects a feedback loop: the preferential rate on capital gains incentivizes holding assets in forms that generate capital gains rather than ordinary income. The lower the rate, the greater the incentive to shift income into capital form. The Income Composition chart below shows this structural shift in stark terms.

Below $200,000 AGI, wages account for more than 80% of all income. Above $10 million, wages collapse to 16%—replaced by capital gains, partnership and S-corporation income, and other investment returns. The tax code's preferential treatment of these income types means the highest earners face effective rates far below what the bracket schedule implies. See: Income Composition by AGI Bin

Section 3The Two Engines of Revenue

Our reform model identifies two distinct revenue channels, and their relative size is surprising.

Channel 1: The Rate Differential (7% of revenue)

The straightforward channel: long-term gains currently taxed at a preferential rate (modeled at 15%) would instead face the filer's ordinary marginal rate (typically 24–37% for high-income filers). In 2021, this rate-differential channel generated $82.6 billion. Substantial, but not the main story.

Channel 2: Stranded Losses (93% of revenue)

The larger and less obvious channel involves what happens to tax-loss harvesting under the reform. Today, investors routinely sell losing positions to generate capital losses that offset their capital gains dollar-for-dollar. In a good year, a portfolio might generate $10 million in gross gains and $9 million in gross losses, leaving only $1 million in net taxable gains at the 15% preferential rate.

Under the reform, those gains become ordinary income. But the corresponding losses are stranded—capital losses can only offset other capital gains or, failing that, a maximum of $3,000 per year of ordinary income under IRC §1211(b). The losses that previously sheltered $9 million from taxation are now nearly worthless. The full $10 million faces taxation at ordinary rates.

In 2021, this stranded-loss channel generated $1,036.7 billion—more than twelve times the rate-differential channel. This is the mechanism that most reform discussions miss entirely.

The preferential rate is not just a lower tax rate. It is the keystone of a loss-harvesting architecture that shelters the vast majority of gross gains from any taxation at all. Removing the preferential rate doesn't just raise the rate—it collapses the shelter.

Section 4Who Pays?

This reform is steeply progressive. In 2021, 78.2% of additional revenue came from filers with AGI above $1 million. The $10 million-plus cohort alone accounted for 49.9% of the total—$558.5 billion from 40,444 returns, an average of $13.8 million per filer.

Section 6How the Brackets Drive It

The existing tax bracket distribution tells us where ordinary income tax revenue comes from today. In 2021, the 22% and 24% brackets generated the largest share of current-law tax revenue—the broad middle of the American tax base. See: Tax Generated by Marginal Rate Bracket

Under the reform, additional revenue concentrates at the top brackets: the 35% and 37% rates. This is because the capital gains being reclassified as ordinary income belong predominantly to filers already in the top brackets. The reform essentially fills in the gap between what those filers would pay on all income at their marginal rate and what they actually pay on their capital gains at 15%.

Section 7The Five-Year Trajectory

The reform's revenue impact moves with capital markets. In the bull market of 2021, when long-term gains reached $1.92 trillion, estimated revenue topped $1.1 trillion. In the 2022 correction, when gains fell to $1.35 trillion, revenue dropped to $718 billion. Our 2023 forecast, based on linear trend projection, estimates $1.0 trillion as gains recover to $1.79 trillion.

Section 8What This Means

The current preferential treatment of capital gains is not merely a lower tax rate. It is the foundation of an architecture that permits the wealthiest Americans to shelter the vast majority of their investment income from taxation through systematic loss harvesting. Our analysis shows that the income currently invisible to the IRS—netted away through strategically realized losses—dwarfs the income that is merely taxed at a lower rate.

Eliminating preferential treatment for gains held fewer than five years would:

  • Generate $500 billion–$1.1 trillion per year depending on market conditions—equivalent to 27–56% of total federal individual income tax collections
  • Draw 73–78% of that revenue from filers with AGI above $1 million
  • Leave filers below $200,000 AGI bearing less than 5% of the additional burden
  • Preserve preferential treatment for truly long-term holdings (5+ years)
  • Substantially reduce the tax incentive for aggressive loss harvesting

A reform of this design is not about punishing investors. It is about recognizing that a one-year holding period is not meaningfully different from ordinary income activity, and that the loss-harvesting architecture it enables has grown far beyond what the preferential rate's original proponents envisioned.

The question is not whether $1.1 trillion in foregone revenue is defensible. The question is whether a tax preference that overwhelmingly benefits 40,000 households—at a cost exceeding the entire federal education budget fourteen times over—represents the best use of the tax code.

How Tax Law Is Changed

The legislative process for federal tax reform
Expand

I Constitutional Foundation

All federal tax legislation must originate in the House of Representatives. Article I, Section 7 of the Constitution provides:

“All Bills for raising Revenue shall originate in the House of Representatives; but the Senate may propose or concur with Amendments as on other Bills.”

This “Origination Clause” ensures that the chamber most directly accountable to voters—with its two-year election cycle—initiates any changes to the tax code. In practice, the Senate routinely amends House-passed revenue bills extensively, but the constitutional starting point remains the House.

II Committee Process

Tax bills are drafted and refined in committee before reaching the floor:

  • House Ways and Means Committee — The primary tax-writing committee in the House. Staff draft the bill in consultation with the Joint Committee on Taxation (JCT), which provides revenue estimates. The committee holds hearings with expert witnesses, then conducts a “markup” session where members debate and amend specific provisions. The bill is reported to the full House with a committee report explaining each provision.
  • House Floor Vote — The Rules Committee sets debate parameters. The House votes on amendments (if allowed) and final passage. A simple majority (218 of 435) is required.

III Senate Consideration

Once the House passes a revenue bill, the Senate has two paths:

  • Regular order — The Senate Finance Committee holds its own hearings and markup, often producing a substantially different bill. Floor debate is subject to the filibuster, requiring 60 votes for cloture.
  • Budget reconciliation — If the tax changes are included in a budget resolution, the bill can pass the Senate with a simple majority (51 votes), bypassing the filibuster. Most major tax legislation since 1981 has used reconciliation, including the Tax Reform Act of 1986, the 2001/2003 Bush tax cuts, and the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017.

The reconciliation path is subject to the “Byrd Rule,” which prohibits provisions that increase the deficit beyond the budget window (typically 10 years) or that are “merely incidental” to the budget. This rule is why many tax provisions include sunset dates.

IV Conference & Presidential Signature

When the House and Senate pass different versions of a tax bill, a conference committee of members from both chambers negotiates a unified text. Both chambers must then approve the conference report without further amendment. The enrolled bill is presented to the President, who may sign it into law or veto it.

V Effective Dates & Transition Rules

Tax legislation almost always specifies an effective date—the date from which new rules apply. Common approaches include:

  • Prospective application — Changes apply only to transactions occurring after the effective date.
  • Grandfathering — Existing positions or holdings are exempt from new rules. For example, assets already held on the date of enactment might retain the prior 1-year holding-period threshold.
  • Phase-in periods — New rates or thresholds are implemented gradually over several years.

The reform modeled in this dashboard assumes prospective application with grandfathering: assets held on the date of enactment retain the current 1-year threshold for long-term treatment, while newly acquired assets face the 5-year threshold.

VI Historical Precedent

Congress has previously adjusted holding-period requirements for capital gains treatment. The Tax Reform Act of 1984 reduced the long-term holding period from “more than 1 year” to “more than 6 months,” and the Tax Reform Act of 1986 restored it to more than 1 year. More recently, §1061, enacted in 2017, imposes a 3-year holding period on “applicable partnership interests” (carried interest) as a condition for long-term capital gains treatment—demonstrating that Congress can and does differentiate holding periods for specific asset categories.

The following sections of the Internal Revenue Code govern the taxation of capital gains. Annotation boxes highlight the specific language that the proposed reform would modify.

B.1 IRC §1222 — Definitions of Capital Gains and Losses

§1222(1) Short-term capital gain. The term “short-term capital gain” means gain from the sale or exchange of a capital asset held for not more than 1 year, if and to the extent such gain is taken into account in computing gross income.
Reform Impact
The threshold “not more than 1 year” would become “not more than 5 years.” Gains on assets held 1–5 years, currently treated as long-term, would be reclassified as short-term and taxed as ordinary income.
§1222(2) Short-term capital loss. The term “short-term capital loss” means loss from the sale or exchange of a capital asset held for not more than 1 year, if and to the extent such loss is taken into account in computing taxable income.
Reform Impact
The threshold becomes “not more than 5 years.” Losses on assets held 1–5 years become short-term losses, which under the reform cannot offset long-term capital gains—they become “stranded.”
§1222(3) Long-term capital gain. The term “long-term capital gain” means gain from the sale or exchange of a capital asset held for more than 1 year, if and to the extent such gain is taken into account in computing gross income.
Reform Impact
The threshold “more than 1 year” would become “more than 5 years.” Only assets held longer than 5 years would qualify for preferential long-term capital gains rates.
§1222(4) Long-term capital loss. The term “long-term capital loss” means loss from the sale or exchange of a capital asset held for more than 1 year, if and to the extent such loss is taken into account in computing taxable income.
Reform Impact
The threshold becomes “more than 5 years.” Only losses on assets held more than 5 years qualify as long-term capital losses.
§1222(5) Net short-term capital gain. The term “net short-term capital gain” means the excess of short-term capital gains for the taxable year over the short-term capital losses for such year.
§1222(6) Net short-term capital loss. The term “net short-term capital loss” means the excess of short-term capital losses for the taxable year over the short-term capital gains for such year.
§1222(7) Net long-term capital gain. The term “net long-term capital gain” means the excess of long-term capital gains for the taxable year over the long-term capital losses for such year.
§1222(8) Net long-term capital loss. The term “net long-term capital loss” means the excess of long-term capital losses for the taxable year over the long-term capital gains for such year.
§1222(9) Net capital gain. The term “net capital gain” means the excess of the net long-term capital gain for the taxable year over the net short-term capital loss for such year.
§1222(10) Net capital loss. The term “net capital loss” means the excess of the losses from sales or exchanges of capital assets over the sum allowed under section 1211. For purposes of determining losses under the preceding sentence, amounts which are short-term capital losses under section 1212 shall be excluded.
§1222(11) Net capital gain. For purposes of section 1(h), the term “net capital gain” means net capital gain (determined without regard to this paragraph) reduced (but not below zero) by the sum of— (A) unrecaptured section 1250 gain, and (B) 28-percent rate gain.
26 U.S.C. §1222 — via Cornell Law Institute (law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/section-1222)

B.2 IRC §1211 — Limitation on Capital Losses

§1211(a) Corporations. In the case of a corporation, losses from sales or exchanges of capital assets shall be allowed only to the extent of gains from such sales or exchanges.
§1211(b) Other taxpayers. In the case of a taxpayer other than a corporation, losses from sales or exchanges of capital assets shall be allowed only to the extent of the gains from such sales or exchanges, plus (if such losses exceed such gains) the lower of— (1) $3,000 ($1,500 in the case of a married individual filing a separate return), or (2) the excess of such losses over such gains.
Reform Impact
Under the reform, short-term capital losses (assets held ≤5 years) cannot offset long-term capital gains. These losses become “stranded”—limited to offsetting other short-term gains or the $3,000 annual deduction against ordinary income. This is the primary revenue driver of the reform: high-income taxpayers currently use short-term losses to shelter long-term gains, and severing that offset generates substantial additional revenue.
26 U.S.C. §1211 — via Cornell Law Institute (law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/section-1211)

B.3 IRC §1223 — Holding Period of Property

Section 1223 provides rules for determining how long a taxpayer has held a capital asset, including “tacking” rules that allow the holding period of one asset to carry over to a replacement asset. Key paragraphs:

§1223(1) In determining the period for which the taxpayer has held property received in an exchange, there shall be included the period for which he held the property exchanged if, under the provisions of section 1031, the property received has, for the purpose of determining gain or loss from a sale or exchange, the same basis in whole or in part in his hands as the property exchanged.
§1223(2) In determining the period for which the taxpayer has held property however acquired there shall be included the period for which such property was held by any other person, if under the provisions of this subtitle such property has, for the purpose of determining gain or loss from a sale or exchange, the same basis in whole or in part in his hands as it would have in the hands of such other person.
§1223(3) In determining the period for which the taxpayer has held stock or securities the acquisition of which (or the contract or option to acquire which) resulted in the nondeductibility (under section 1091 relating to wash sales) of the loss from the sale or other disposition of substantially identical stock or securities, there shall be included the period for which he held the stock or securities the loss from the sale or other disposition of which was not deductible.
§1223(9) In the case of a person acquiring property from a decedent or to whom property passed from a decedent (within the meaning of section 1014(b)), if— (A) the basis of such property in the hands of such person is determined under section 1014, and (B) such property is sold or otherwise disposed of by such person within 1 year after the decedent’s date of death, then such person shall be considered to have held such property for more than 1 year.
Reform Impact
Paragraph (9) creates a deemed holding period for inherited property. Under the reform, the “1 year” references would become “5 years,” so inherited property sold within 1 year would be deemed held for more than 5 years. The grandfathering provision ensures inherited property from decedents dying before enactment retains the current treatment.
§1223(10) If— (A) property is acquired by any person in a transfer to which section 1040 applies, (B) such property is sold or otherwise disposed of by such person within 1 year after the decedent’s date of death, and (C) such sale or disposition is to a person who is a qualified heir (as defined in section 2032A(e)(1)) with respect to the decedent, then the person making such sale or other disposition shall be considered to have held such property for more than 1 year.
26 U.S.C. §1223 — via Cornell Law Institute (law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/section-1223)

B.4 IRC §1(h) — Preferential Capital Gains Rates

Section 1(h) is the operative provision that taxes “net capital gain” (as defined in §1222(11)) at preferential rates rather than the ordinary income rates. The rate tiers are:

  • 0% — for net capital gain that would otherwise be taxed at the 10% or 12% ordinary rate
  • 15% — for net capital gain that would otherwise be taxed at the 22%, 24%, 32%, or 35% ordinary rate
  • 20% — for net capital gain that would otherwise be taxed at the 37% ordinary rate
  • 25% — for unrecaptured section 1250 gain (depreciation recapture on real property)
  • 28% — for collectibles gain and certain small business stock gain (§1202)
Key Mechanism
Section 1(h) does not independently define “long-term” or “short-term.” It relies on the definitions in §1222, which in turn rely on the holding-period rules in §1223. By changing the threshold in §1222 from “more than 1 year” to “more than 5 years,” the preferential rates in §1(h) automatically become available only for assets held more than 5 years. No amendment to §1(h) itself is required.

This cascading design is a feature of the Internal Revenue Code: definitions flow through cross-references, so a targeted change to §1222 has broad downstream effects without needing to amend dozens of other provisions.

26 U.S.C. §1(h) — via Cornell Law Institute (law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/section-1)

(b) Grandfathering. — In the case of any capital asset held by the taxpayer on the date of the enactment of this Act, the amendments made by this Act shall not apply, and such asset shall be treated under the provisions of sections 1222 and 1223 of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986 as in effect before the date of the enactment of this Act. (c) Identification. — The Secretary shall prescribe such regulations as may be necessary to require adequate identification of assets subject to the grandfathering rule under subsection (b), including rules for determining the holding period of fungible assets.

Architecture & Methodology

Data pipeline from IRS source files to interactive dashboard
Expand

The diagram below traces data from IRS Statistics of Income source files through five processing stages to the interactive charts in this dashboard. Hover any node or edge for implementation details.

Stage 1 — Download. The download.py script fetches 63 files from irs.gov/pub/irs-soi: 55 Pub 1304 Excel workbooks (11 per tax year across 2018–2022), 5 Revenue Procedure PDFs, and 3 Sales of Capital Assets (SOCA) Excel workbooks (Table 4 for TY2013–2015). Each file is verified against a SHA-256 checksum manifest. Pub 1304 files use the legacy .xls (BIFF) format; SOCA files use .xlsx.

Stage 2 — Parse & Load. The pipeline orchestrator (pipeline.py) calls four specialized parsers. parse_table_1x handles Tables 1.1, 1.2, 3.2, and 3.3 (return counts, AGI, taxable income, and tax items). parse_table_14a handles Table 1.4A (Schedule D capital gains detail: ST/LT gains, ST/LT losses, loss carryovers, and net capital gains by AGI bin—the loss and carryover columns enable calibration validation). parse_table_3x handles Tables 3.4–3.6 (tax generated at each marginal rate bracket by filing status). parse_soca handles SOCA Table 4 (long-term gains by holding duration and asset type, using openpyxl for the .xlsx format). All Pub 1304 parsers use xlrd to read BIFF-format files, strip footnote markers, and multiply monetary values by 1,000. Parsed rows are loaded into 9 raw tables in a SQLite database.

Stage 3 — SQL Views. The schema.sql file defines three layers of views on top of the raw tables. Four canonical views present the data model: AGI_BINS (19 income brackets), RETURNS_AGGREGATE (return counts and tax by bin and filing status), CAPITAL_GAINS (short-term and long-term gains, losses, and carryovers by bin), and BRACKET_DISTRIBUTION (tax per marginal rate). Three CPI-adjusted views convert nominal dollars to constant 2022 dollars using Bureau of Labor Statistics CPI-U annual averages. Seven validation views enable cross-table reconciliation checks.

Stage 4 — Export & Validate. export_data.py reads the canonical views into DataFrames, runs the five-step microsimulation reform pipeline (identify affected gains, reclassify as ordinary income, compute rate differential, estimate stranded losses, sum net revenue), and writes dashboard.json. Two validation modules run independently: reconcile.py runs 6 cross-table validation checks against the validation views, ensuring all overlapping aggregates match within 0.05% tolerance; calibration_validate.py compares empirical loss ratios (computed from Table 1.4A loss and carryover columns) and holding period fractions (computed from SOCA Table 4 duration bins) against the model’s assumed calibration values in reform_assumptions.json. Parquet files are also exported in both nominal and CPI-adjusted formats.

Stage 5 — Dashboard. This single-page HTML dashboard fetches dashboard.json at page load and renders seven interactive Chart.js visualizations. All computation happens server-side in Python; the browser only handles presentation and interactivity.

Model Assumptions

Calibration values for holding periods and loss harvesting
Expand

The reform model relies on two key calibration parameters that cannot be observed directly in IRS Statistics of Income data. Holding period fractions estimate what share of reported long-term gains were held between one and five years—the gains that would lose their preferential rate under the reform. Loss harvesting ratios estimate how much of each AGI group’s gross long-term gains are offset by intentionally realized losses before appearing as the net figures in SOI tables. Together, these two parameters determine the reform’s tax base: the holding period fraction selects which gains are affected, and the loss ratio reveals the hidden gross gains that become fully taxable once stranded-loss rules apply.

Both parameters vary by income level. The values below are applied in Step 3 (holding period filter) and Step 2 (gross gain estimation) of the five-step reform pipeline described in the methodology footer.

What the published data shows. IRS SOI Table 1.4A (2018–2022) publishes net long-term capital gains and losses by AGI group. For the $10 M+ group, effective tax rates drop (from 27.5% at $5–10 M to 25.1% at $10 M+) because 47% of their AGI consists of long-term capital gains taxed at 23.8% instead of the 37% top ordinary rate. This preferential-rate gap is the mechanism the reform targets.

Why the gross capital-loss percentage is not included. Table 1.4A columns 62 and 64 report per-return net long-term gains and losses—not gross transaction-level amounts. A filer with $500K of gross gains and $400K of harvested losses appears as just $100K in column 62; the $400K of harvested activity is invisible. Furthermore, for the $10 M+ group, only 28% of net LT gains come from direct sales (where individual transactions are visible). The remaining 72% flows through partnerships, S corporations, and other pass-through forms that report only a single net K-1 figure—all internal trading and loss harvesting within hedge funds, private equity funds, and similar vehicles is netted away before it reaches the individual return. As a result, the observable “implied loss ratios” from Table 1.4A (5–84% by AGI group) run in the opposite direction from expected harvesting intensity, showing low ratios for high-income filers precisely because their harvesting is the most thoroughly hidden by per-return and partnership-level netting. The gross loss ratios assumed below are structural estimates that cannot be validated from published Schedule D aggregates alone.

Holding Period Fractions

Fraction of LT gains held 1–5 years (remainder held 5+). Applied in Step 3 to determine which gains lose preferential treatment.

Loss Harvesting Offset

Fraction of gross LT gains offset by harvested losses. At 0.85, only 15% survives as net gains. Applied in Step 2 to reconstruct gross gains from SOI net figures.

Sources: IRS SOI Capital Assets Study (2007–2012), JCT estimates. Holding period fractions cross-checked against SOCA Table 4 holding duration bins (TY2015); the aggregate fraction (37.9% at 1–5 yr) brackets the model’s assumed range (20–55%). Loss harvesting ratios are structural estimates informed by the same studies. Published Table 1.4A data (2018–2022) confirms the overall gain-vs-loss pattern by AGI but cannot validate the gross ratios directly, for the netting and pass-through reasons described above. Run python -m src.validation.calibration_validate for the full comparison report.

Relevant Data

Key statistics and IRS source references
Expand

Every number in this dashboard traces back to IRS Statistics of Income (SOI) data. The primary source is Publication 1304, the most detailed publicly available breakdown of individual income tax returns. The model ingests seven SOI table families—covering income by AGI bracket, filing status, capital asset transactions, and marginal rate distributions—for tax years 2018 through 2022, with a trend-based 2023 forecast. Additionally, the Sales of Capital Assets (SOCA) study provides holding period duration data for TY2013–2015 used in calibration validation. These tables supply the raw inputs to the five-step reform pipeline: AGI and return counts establish population weights, Table 1.4A provides the capital gains base (including loss and carryover columns for calibration), and Tables 3.4–3.6 anchor the bracket-based marginal rate calculation.
