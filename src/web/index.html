The $1.1 Trillion Question: Why Capital Gains Tax Reform Can't Wait
Expand

Every year, the wealthiest Americans legally eliminate hundreds of billions of dollars in capital gains from their tax obligations. Not through exotic offshore schemes or illegal tax evasion—but through a structural feature of the tax code that rewards sophisticated portfolio management with a tax rate roughly half what ordinary workers pay. This analysis quantifies that gap and shows what closing it would look like.

Section 1The Scale of the Problem

In tax year 2021, American taxpayers reported $1.92 trillion in net long-term capital gains on 20.5 million Schedule D filings—a record driven by surging equity and real estate markets. That figure alone exceeds the GDP of all but a handful of nations. But it's the number you don't see on the IRS Statistics of Income tables that tells the real story.

Our model estimates that before tax-loss harvesting, gross long-term gains were substantially larger than the net figures reported to the IRS. Across all income levels, investors strategically realize losses to offset gains, reducing their taxable capital income. This is perfectly legal. It is also enormously expensive for the public fisc.

$1.1 trillion Estimated additional federal revenue in TY2021 from eliminating preferential treatment for gains held under 5 years

That number—over one trillion dollars in a single year—is not a theoretical maximum. It is the result of a conservative, five-step microsimulation applied to actual IRS data at all 19 income levels, using real tax bracket schedules and calibrated loss ratios. It accounts for the $3,000 annual cap on capital loss deductions against ordinary income (IRC §1211(b)), the composition of gains by holding period, and the return-count-weighted marginal rates across all filing statuses. Even in the 2022 correction, when gains fell to $1.35 trillion, the reform still generated $718 billion.

Section 2Where the Money Is

The concentration of capital gains income in America is extreme, and growing. In 2021, filers with AGI above $2 million held 61.2% of all long-term gains, despite representing a tiny fraction of total filers. The $10 million-and-above cohort alone held 41.9% of all long-term gains—$805.6 billion in net reported gains from just 40,444 returns. See: Capital Gains Concentration

40,444 filers reported $805.6 billion in long-term gains. That is $19.9 million per return— more than most Americans will earn in a lifetime.

This concentration is not merely an artifact of income inequality. It reflects a feedback loop: the preferential rate on capital gains incentivizes holding assets in forms that generate capital gains rather than ordinary income. The lower the rate, the greater the incentive to shift income into capital form. The Income Composition chart below shows this structural shift in stark terms.

Below $200,000 AGI, wages account for more than 80% of all income. Above $10 million, wages collapse to 16%—replaced by capital gains, partnership and S-corporation income, and other investment returns. The tax code's preferential treatment of these income types means the highest earners face effective rates far below what the bracket schedule implies. See: Income Composition by AGI Bin

Section 3The Two Engines of Revenue

Our reform model identifies two distinct revenue channels, and their relative size is surprising.

Channel 1: The Rate Differential (7% of revenue)

The straightforward channel: long-term gains currently taxed at a preferential rate (modeled at 15%) would instead face the filer's ordinary marginal rate (typically 24–37% for high-income filers). In 2021, this rate-differential channel generated $82.6 billion. Substantial, but not the main story.

Channel 2: Stranded Losses (93% of revenue)

The larger and less obvious channel involves what happens to tax-loss harvesting under the reform. Today, investors routinely sell losing positions to generate capital losses that offset their capital gains dollar-for-dollar. In a good year, a portfolio might generate $10 million in gross gains and $9 million in gross losses, leaving only $1 million in net taxable gains at the 15% preferential rate.

Under the reform, those gains become ordinary income. But the corresponding losses are stranded—capital losses can only offset other capital gains or, failing that, a maximum of $3,000 per year of ordinary income under IRC §1211(b). The losses that previously sheltered $9 million from taxation are now nearly worthless. The full $10 million faces taxation at ordinary rates.

In 2021, this stranded-loss channel generated $1,036.7 billion—more than twelve times the rate-differential channel. This is the mechanism that most reform discussions miss entirely.

The preferential rate is not just a lower tax rate. It is the keystone of a loss-harvesting architecture that shelters the vast majority of gross gains from any taxation at all. Removing the preferential rate doesn't just raise the rate—it collapses the shelter.

Section 4Who Pays?

This reform is steeply progressive. In 2021, 78.2% of additional revenue came from filers with AGI above $1 million. The $10 million-plus cohort alone accounted for 49.9% of the total—$558.5 billion from 40,444 returns, an average of $13.8 million per filer.

Section 5The Filing Status Story

Breaking capital gains income by filing status reveals another dimension of concentration. Married filing jointly dominates at every income level, reflecting the reality that high-net-worth capital gains income is overwhelmingly held by joint filers. The chart below shows how long-term capital gains are distributed across AGI groups and filing statuses.

The pattern is clear: the vast majority of capital gains income accrues to joint filers in the highest AGI brackets. Single filers and head-of-household filers—a proxy for single parents—hold only a small fraction. This is not a reform that falls on working families. It falls on joint-filing households with multi-million-dollar investment portfolios executing sophisticated tax strategies. See: Reform Revenue by Filing Status

Section 6How the Brackets Drive It

The existing tax bracket distribution tells us where ordinary income tax revenue comes from today. In 2021, the 22% and 24% brackets generated the largest share of current-law tax revenue—the broad middle of the American tax base. See: Tax Generated by Marginal Rate Bracket

Under the reform, additional revenue concentrates at the top brackets: the 35% and 37% rates. This is because the capital gains being reclassified as ordinary income belong predominantly to filers already in the top brackets. The reform essentially fills in the gap between what those filers would pay on all income at their marginal rate and what they actually pay on their capital gains at 15%.

Section 7The Five-Year Trajectory

The reform's revenue impact moves with capital markets. In the bull market of 2021, when long-term gains reached $1.92 trillion, estimated revenue topped $1.1 trillion. In the 2022 correction, when gains fell to $1.35 trillion, revenue dropped to $718 billion. Our 2023 forecast, based on linear trend projection, estimates $1.0 trillion as gains recover to $1.79 trillion.

Second, even in a down year (2022), the reform generates more than $700 billion, providing a substantial and relatively stable revenue base.

Section 8What This Means

The current preferential treatment of capital gains is not merely a lower tax rate. It is the foundation of an architecture that permits the wealthiest Americans to shelter the vast majority of their investment income from taxation through systematic loss harvesting. Our analysis shows that the income currently invisible to the IRS—netted away through strategically realized losses—dwarfs the income that is merely taxed at a lower rate.

Eliminating preferential treatment for gains held fewer than five years would:

  • Generate $500 billion–$1.1 trillion per year depending on market conditions
  • Draw 73–78% of that revenue from filers with AGI above $1 million
  • Leave filers below $200,000 AGI bearing less than 5% of the additional burden
  • Preserve preferential treatment for truly long-term holdings (5+ years)
  • Substantially reduce the tax incentive for aggressive loss harvesting

A reform of this design is not about punishing investors. It is about recognizing that a one-year holding period is not meaningfully different from ordinary income activity, and that the loss-harvesting architecture it enables has grown far beyond what the preferential rate's original proponents envisioned.

The question is not whether $1.1 trillion in foregone revenue is defensible. The question is whether a tax preference that overwhelmingly benefits 40,000 households—at a cost exceeding the entire federal education budget fourteen times over—represents the best use of the tax code.

Methodology: Microsimulation using IRS SOI Publication 1304 data, Tax Years 2018–2022. TY2023 values are linear trend-based forecasts; actual SOI data expected March 2026. Five-step pipeline: (1) cross-category loss netting, (2) gross gain estimation from calibrated loss ratios, (3) holding period filter, (4) bracket-based marginal rate weighted by filing status return counts, (5) revenue with stranded-loss accounting per IRC §1211(b). Static estimate; does not model behavioral responses, realization elasticities, or general equilibrium effects.

Limitations: Holding period fractions and gross loss ratios are calibration placeholders based on IRS SOI Capital Assets Study (2007–2012) and JCT estimates. Actual behavioral response to the reform (reduced harvesting, deferred realization, asset reallocation) would reduce revenue. This is an upper-bound static estimate, not a dynamic score.

Glossary of Terms

Definitions, chart explanations, and methodology
Expand

Tax & Model Terms

AGIAdjusted Gross Income; total income minus above-the-line deductions
Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG)Gains on assets held more than one year; currently taxed at preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20%)
Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG)Gains on assets held one year or less; taxed as ordinary income
Preferential RateThe reduced tax rate (modeled at 15%) applied to LTCG under current law
Marginal Tax RateThe rate applied to the last dollar of taxable income within a bracket
Effective Tax RateTotal tax divided by total income; the average rate actually paid
Filing StatusIRS classification (Single, Married Filing Jointly, Married Filing Separately, Head of Household) that determines bracket thresholds
Schedule DIRS form for reporting capital gains and losses
Stranded LossesCapital losses that can no longer offset gains after reclassification; limited to $3,000/year deduction against ordinary income (IRC §1211(b))
Rate Differential RevenueAdditional tax from applying the ordinary marginal rate instead of the 15% preferential rate to reclassified gains
CPI-U AdjustmentConsumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers; used to convert nominal dollars to constant 2022 dollars
