The $1.1 Trillion Question: Why Capital Gains Tax Reform Can't Wait

A microsimulation analysis of IRS Statistics of Income data, Tax Years 2018–2023

Every year, the wealthiest Americans legally eliminate hundreds of billions of dollars in capital gains from their tax obligations. Not through exotic offshore schemes or illegal tax evasion—but through a structural feature of the tax code that rewards sophisticated portfolio management with a tax rate roughly half what ordinary workers pay. This analysis quantifies that gap and shows what closing it would look like.

Section 1The Scale of the Problem

In tax year 2022, American taxpayers reported $1.35 trillion in net long-term capital gains on 12.9 million Schedule D filings. That figure alone is staggering—roughly equal to the entire GDP of Australia. But it's the number you don't see on the IRS Statistics of Income tables that tells the real story. See: Year-over-Year Trends

Our model estimates that before tax-loss harvesting, gross long-term gains were substantially larger than the net figures reported to the IRS. Across all income levels, investors strategically realize losses to offset gains, reducing their taxable capital income. This is perfectly legal. It is also enormously expensive for the public fisc.

$717.6 billion Estimated additional federal revenue in TY2022 from eliminating preferential treatment for gains held under 5 years

That number—$718 billion in a single year—is not a theoretical maximum. It is the result of a conservative, five-step microsimulation applied to actual IRS data at all 19 income levels, using real tax bracket schedules and calibrated loss ratios. It accounts for the $3,000 annual cap on capital loss deductions against ordinary income (IRC §1211(b)), the composition of gains by holding period, and the return-count-weighted marginal rates across all filing statuses.

Section 2Where the Money Is

The concentration of capital gains income in America is extreme, and growing. In 2022, filers with AGI above $2 million held 56.7% of all long-term gains, despite representing a tiny fraction of total filers. The $10 million-and-above cohort alone held 36.6% of all long-term gains—$494.7 billion in net reported gains from just 25,066 returns. See: Capital Gains Concentration

25,066 filers reported $494.7 billion in long-term gains. That is $19.7 million per return— more than most Americans will earn in a lifetime.

This concentration is not merely an artifact of income inequality. It reflects a feedback loop: the preferential rate on capital gains incentivizes holding assets in forms that generate capital gains rather than ordinary income. The lower the rate, the greater the incentive to shift income into capital form. The result is a tax system that collects less from those with the greatest ability to pay. See: Who Bears the Burden?

Section 3The Two Engines of Revenue

Our reform model identifies two distinct revenue channels, and their relative size is surprising.

Channel 1: The Rate Differential (7–8% of revenue)

The straightforward channel: long-term gains currently taxed at a preferential rate (modeled at 15%) would instead face the filer's ordinary marginal rate (typically 24–37% for high-income filers). In 2022, this rate-differential channel generated $53.8 billion. Substantial, but not the main story. See: Revenue Impact by Income Segment

Channel 2: Stranded Losses (92–93% of revenue)

The larger and less obvious channel involves what happens to tax-loss harvesting under the reform. Today, investors routinely sell losing positions to generate capital losses that offset their capital gains dollar-for-dollar. In a good year, a portfolio might generate $10 million in gross gains and $9 million in gross losses, leaving only $1 million in net taxable gains at the 15% preferential rate.

Under the reform, those gains become ordinary income. But the corresponding losses are stranded—capital losses can only offset other capital gains or, failing that, a maximum of $3,000 per year of ordinary income under IRC §1211(b). The losses that previously sheltered $9 million from taxation are now nearly worthless. The full $10 million faces taxation at ordinary rates.

In 2022, this stranded-loss channel generated $664.6 billion—twelve times the rate-differential channel. This is the mechanism that most reform discussions miss entirely.

The preferential rate is not just a lower tax rate. It is the keystone of a loss-harvesting architecture that shelters the vast majority of gross gains from any taxation at all. Removing the preferential rate doesn't just raise the rate—it collapses the shelter.

Section 4Who Pays?

This reform is steeply progressive. In 2022, 76.6% of additional revenue came from filers with AGI above $1 million. The $10 million-plus cohort alone accounted for 46.3% of the total—$332 billion from 25,066 returns, an average of $13.3 million per filer. See: Who Bears the Burden?

The reason for this concentration is mechanical: the wealthiest filers have the largest portfolios, harvest losses most aggressively (their gross loss ratios approach 90%), and face the highest marginal rates (37%). Every dollar of previously sheltered gain faces the steepest possible ordinary tax rate. Filers below $200,000 bear just 1.5% of the additional burden.

Section 5The Filing Status Story

Breaking reform revenue by filing status reveals another dimension of concentration. Married filing jointly accounts for 77.8% of additional revenue ($558 billion in 2022), reflecting the reality that high-net-worth capital gains income is overwhelmingly held by joint filers. Single filers contribute 14.6%, while head-of-household filers—a proxy for single parents—contribute just 2.8%. See: Reform Revenue by Filing Status

This is not a reform that falls on working families. It falls on joint-filing households with multi-million-dollar investment portfolios executing sophisticated tax strategies.

Section 6How the Brackets Drive It

The existing tax bracket distribution tells us where ordinary income tax revenue comes from today. In 2022, the 22% and 24% brackets generated the largest share of current-law tax revenue—the broad middle of the American tax base. See: Tax Generated by Marginal Rate Bracket

Under the reform, additional revenue concentrates at the top brackets: the 35% and 37% rates. This is because the capital gains being reclassified as ordinary income belong predominantly to filers already in the top brackets. The reform essentially fills in the gap between what those filers would pay on all income at their marginal rate and what they actually pay on their capital gains at 15%.

Section 7The Five-Year Trajectory

The reform's revenue impact moves with capital markets. In the bull market of 2021, when long-term gains reached $1.92 trillion, estimated revenue topped $1.1 trillion. In the 2022 correction, when gains fell to $1.35 trillion, revenue dropped to $718 billion. Our 2023 forecast, based on linear trend projection, estimates $1.0 trillion as gains recover to $1.79 trillion. See: Year-over-Year Trends

Section 8What This Means

The current preferential treatment of capital gains is not merely a lower tax rate. It is the foundation of an architecture that permits the wealthiest Americans to shelter the vast majority of their investment income from taxation through systematic loss harvesting. Our analysis shows that the income currently invisible to the IRS—netted away through strategically realized losses—dwarfs the income that is merely taxed at a lower rate.

Eliminating preferential treatment for gains held fewer than five years would:

  • Generate $500–1,100 billion per year depending on market conditions
  • Draw 73–78% of that revenue from filers with AGI above $1 million
  • Leave filers below $200,000 AGI bearing less than 2% of the additional burden
  • Preserve preferential treatment for truly long-term holdings (5+ years)
  • Substantially reduce the tax incentive for aggressive loss harvesting

A reform of this design is not about punishing investors. It is about recognizing that a one-year holding period is not meaningfully different from ordinary income activity, and that the loss-harvesting architecture it enables has grown far beyond what the preferential rate's original proponents envisioned.

The question is not whether $718 billion in foregone revenue is defensible. The question is whether a tax preference that overwhelmingly benefits 25,000 households—at a cost exceeding the entire federal education budget ten times over—represents the best use of the tax code.

Interpretation

The calibration reveals a striking pattern: the initial loss ratio predictions were reasonable for low-income groups (Under $30K: predicted 0.70, calibrated 0.84) but significantly overstated for high-income groups ($5M+: predicted 0.90, calibrated 0.05). This is because high-income filers receive the majority of their long-term gains from partnership/S-corporation K-1 flows and capital gain distributions, where gains vastly exceed losses. In TY2020, filers above $5M AGI received $328B in partnership LT gains against only $7B in partnership LT losses—a 47:1 gain-to-loss ratio. By contrast, low-income filers rely primarily on direct sales of capital assets, where loss harvesting is more prevalent relative to gain volume. The model’s initial predictions inverted this relationship, assuming that higher-income filers harvest more aggressively. The data shows they do harvest in absolute terms, but their gain base is so large that losses represent a much smaller fraction of total gross activity.

Calibration Approaches — Methodology Detail: Five approaches were investigated to derive calibration parameters from published IRS data. Each produced bounds rather than point estimates; the three most informative were combined into the “3-Approach Calibrated” values shown above.

  1. Component reconstruction (Table 1.4A, 120 columns). Reconstructs gross LT activity from four transaction sub-categories (Form 8949 boxes A–D), partnership/S-corp K-1 flows, other-form gains (4797, 8824, etc.), and capital gain distributions. Partnership gains are expanded by income-graduated internal netting rates (5% for Under $30K to 40% for $5M+) to estimate hidden losses netted before K-1 reporting. In TY2020: gross LT gain from sales $493B, partnership LT gain $291B, other-form LT gain $289B, capital gain distributions $56B. Used as the primary calibrated estimate (Approach 1 in the pipeline).
  2. Non-taxable gain adjustment (Table 1.4A × High-Income PDF cross-check). Table 1.4A reports “Taxable net gain”—the subset of filers whose net gain increased tax liability. Cross-checking against SOI High-Income data revealed ~10% of gain filers are non-taxable but carry only ~0.5% of gain dollars; loss data matched exactly. A 0.5% upward adjustment to gains produces a small downward shift (~0.3%) in the loss ratio. Used as a correction factor applied to Approach 1 (Approach 2 in the pipeline).
  3. Coverage bounds from PDF validation. For $200K+ bins (high confidence): gain undercount ≤0.5%, producing tight empirical bounds. For <$200K bins (medium confidence): wider bounds allowing up to 2% undercount. Loss data is exact across all bins. Used as clipping bounds on the combined estimate (Approach 3 in the pipeline).
  4. Sales price and cost basis reconstruction (Table 1.4A, cols 70–72, 78–80, 88–90, 98–100). TY2020 LT proceeds were $3.2T against $2.6T basis. Limitation: the “no basis reported” category has unreliable basis figures, and the data excludes passthrough flows and distributions ($346B net LT gain in TY2020). Provides a directional check but was not incorporated into the combined estimate.
  5. Loss carryover stock as harvesting proxy (Table 1.4A, cols 117–118). LT loss carryover was $278B in TY2020 against $19.3B in annual taxable net loss—a 14× ratio. Confirms large latent loss supply but is a cumulative balance, not an annual flow. Not incorporated into the combined estimate.

Limitations: This is an upper-bound static estimate, not a dynamic score. Actual behavioral response to the reform (reduced harvesting, deferred realization, asset reallocation) would reduce revenue. The SOCA holding period data is from 2015 (most recent available); structural ratios are assumed stable over the 2018–2022 analysis period. SOCA does not provide AGI-stratified holding period breakdowns, so a single aggregate fraction is applied with model-assumed AGI variation.
